What Could Go Wrong? - Building a Secure Application

Information security is important to app software developers. We need to appreciate the risks and take steps to mitigate them. We need to develop apps with security in mind from the beginning, not as a last minute add. Developers must incorporate security into the architecture, not apply it to the facade. The purpose of this blog post is to make you the reader security-conscious, to understand a few of the ways that adversaries can compromise an application. This blog post does not provide remedial guidance; rather it provides resources for developers to learn more about the security landscape and to educate themselves on what they can do.

No application is completely secure. Given enough time and resources, adversaries can compromise anything. The best you can hope for is to harden your defenses so that attackers go after someone else. Adversaries target low-hanging fruit. You don't have to build a perfectly secure app, merely one that dissuades most attackers from trying. If you and your friend are being chased by a bear, you don't have to outrun the bear. You only have to outrun your friend.

Although Apple products have a reputation for being the most secure, they are not impregnable. Apple's growing populatity has attracted the attention of adversaries, and they have begun developing malware that specifically targets Apple phones and devices.

Threat Modeling:

Threat modeling is a process of identifying objectives and risks and taking steps to mitigate them. Threat modeling is done both IT networks as well as applications, such as the ones we are building. The first step in threat modeling is determining vulnerabilities: What does the app do? How do people interact with it, and how does it interact with the rest of the software on the iPhone / iPad? Is there anything in your app that an attacker might want? How would an attacker get into the app. "Modern threat modeling looks at a system from a potential attacker's perspective, as opposed to a defender's point of view."

Data

One of the top things that attackers want is data. Think of all the data that your app might touch. Someone might be able to determine the user's identity. They might be able to compromise the password used to access the app. And given how frequently people recycle passwords, that password might unlock other accounts. Your app may reference financial or medical information, it may reveal that user's name and phone number.

Beachhead

In addition to stealing information stored in your app, hackers may also be interested in using your app as a beachhead. The word beachhead is a military term, and it refers to the temporary area controlled by invading forces on an enemy beach. Enemy forces establish a beachhead from which they will later break out and take over even more land. In iOS development, attackers can use an app as a beachhead into other sectors of a phone. For instance, they can try to gain access to sensitive information in your phone, such as your contacts, text messages, other apps' stored data. For example, some apps grant automatic access to phone calls, text messages, roaming data and payments, which a hacker can exploit to access the phone user's financial resources. Or the malware could force the phone to sign up for premium rate text masseges or phone calls.
